# Conflict Resolution
Conflict is inevitable in any consistent group of people. It's best to have structures in place for how to manage conflict in a healthy way. Conflict is often seen as a bad or dangerous thing, but with the right approach conflict can actually be a regenerative and positive way to grow.

This document covers concepts on how we might handle conflict resolution as a group.

The primary approach covered here is [NVC](https://www.cultureally.com/blog/nonviolent-communication-a-guide-to-empathetic-conversation) (Non-violent Communication) which is centered around compassionate connection.

Conflict is different from disagreement. Disagreement is about ideas, conflict is when disagreement starts affecting feelings and relationship. That being said, the tools here can still be helpful in resolving disagreement. In general, disagreements should be resolved via the consensus method as stated in the governanace structure.

## Handling Conflict
If you are confortable engaging in healthy conflict this section covers some guidelines on how to engage conflict in a positive way.

NVC's core structure has four main components:
1. Observation - observe rather than judge or evaluate. Try not to start from a place of criticism, but from needs, empathy, and concern. Explain the situation without making assumptions about the other parties intent or feelings.
2. Feelings - state feelings, try and do so from a general and internal perspective rather than an external one. e.g. don't say "you're making me feel X!" instead try "When someone does Y it makes me feel X".  
3. Needs - clearly identify what the need is behind the feeling.
4. Requests - A concrete do-able ask, not a demand. This is the point where the parties try to compromise to find a way to fulfill everyone's needs.

When engaging in conflict, there is a ritual that can be very helpful to either state out loud (by a mediator or by the parties) or to just hold in your minds. An initial common ground agreement for the parties to try and reach consensus: 

"We agree to do our best to work through this together without blame or judgement."

This allows the parties to start on common ground.  It's also important to recognize that conflict is uncomfortable and often doesn't feel very good. That's ok! Just know that the discomfort is a part of the process.

Then the parties take turns observing, stating feelings, and stating needs. At no point in this process should the parties make statements or assumptions about the other parties intentions or feelings. "I don't know why you didn't respond" is fine; "you didn't respond because you don't respect my time" is not. They do this without interruption or rebuttal from the other party. We are not trying to prove who is right or wrong but to meet needs and respect boundaries. 

Then each party takes turns reflecting what they heard from the other. e.g. "I'm hearing that action X makes you feel anxious, is that right?" This is a time to get curious, not to make assumptions or try and prove a point. Do your absolute best to try and understand what the other party is feeling and why. 

Once this is done, the parties take turns stating requests that they think would be acceptable to everyone involved. Work together to find acceptable compromises. Like in the observations step, requests should be phrased without judgement but should instead be focused on needs.

At any point in the process either party or the mediator can call for a break if things seem like they are getting too heated or anyone needs a moment.

There should be a final moment where each party verbally agrees to accept the requests and resolution.

If the party can't reach a resolution or gets stuck they should seek out a mediator.
