# Governance Structure
This page documents our governanace structure. How we run the group as a whole and how we manage projects.

We currently operate thanks to the support of the CRASH Space Makerspace. Before getting into the details, it's important to note that following this handbook is only necessary for the support of the LA Solarpunk group. CRASH is a do-ocracy so even if the Solarpunk group decides not to support a project people have the right to go to and work with CRASH on their own.

With that being said, we'll go over group governance and then project lifecycles.

## Group Governanace
As a group we are essentially hosting many different projects. Hosting a project means that we:
- provide volunteer support
- give the project visibility within the group
- advocate for the project externally
- do what we can to provide funds and source parts where necessary

We operate on principals of [lazy consensus](https://community.apache.org/committers/decisionMaking.html) and do-ocracy. That means that anyone can propose something within the group and as long as no-one objects within 72 hours the proposal is accepted. 

There are some hard requirements for proposals:
- If a proposal is made to do work with for-profit entities it can be vetoed by any active member of the group. 
- Proposals must include requirements and have a well defined end-point.
- Proposals must comply with the mission statement. Any member can bring up concerns here to move the proposal to a vote

An active member is defined as anyone who has been to at least 2 meetings in the last 90 days.

If an objection is raised, the group can discuss the objection. If the objection remains unresolved, the proposal moves to a vote. Members can vote over the next 7 days for or against the proposal. At least 10 members must vote and the proposal only carries forward if at least 66% of those who vote approve. Whoever made the objection is responsible for initiating the vote and collecting the results. If no vote mechanism is put forth in the next 7 days, the initial proposal proceeds. 

If the proposal proceeds after objection either through abstention or from a successful vote the objection should be acknowledged although it doesn't have to be resolved.

If a project is successfully proposed it becomes an official LA Solarpunk project.

## Project Lifecycle

After a project is proposed, someone is chosen as the facilitator of that project (usually the person who proposed it). If multiple people would like the role they can either co-facilitate or if they'd prefer only one they can have the LA Solarpunk group as a whole vote on it.

The roles of the facilitator(s) are:
- Facilitates day-to-day calls on direction, scope, and priority without needing to convene anyone
- Is the point of contact with the partner org (if it's a partnership project)
- Has tie-breaking authority if consensus genuinely deadlocks after the time-box runs out
- Facilitates internal communication with anyone on the project
- Handles scheduling build days, meetups, etc.

Any active LA Solarpunk member can join a project as a collaborator. And anyone on the project can make a proposal for that project through the lazy consensus do-ocracy model. When proposing it's always recommended to discuss with the group wherever possible first before trying to move forward with work on it.

Once a project is established, the members of that project can make decisions independently of the larger LA Solarpunk group. The larger group can still step in if it's determined that the project no longer adheres to the mission or ideals of LA Solarpunk. If this happens, the people on the project are still free to continue, but they do so without support of LA Solarpunk.

Work continues until the defined end-point is reached at which point the project is either considered closed or moves to maintenance.


## Working With External Partners
One of the main goals of this group is to do work with local organizations, mutual aid groups, and non-profits that align with the mission of LA solarpunk. There are some guidelines when working with partners to ensure we are effective and respectful in how we work with them:
- There should be a post-work plan in place for the partner. This may include continued operations support, documentation, and instructions on maintaining or working with the contribution.
- Talk with the partner to address real problems they're experiencing vs just assuming problems they may be having. It's best if the problem and the solution are measurable.
- The partner get's to decide what we build for them, make sure they want the thing you're proposing before you build it.
- Be respectful of how the partner operates, unless you're a member of that organization or they've explicitly asked for feedback, we really shouldn't be telling them how to run things.
- Be patient. A lot of these orgs are often running on razor thin margins and are often very overworked. Do whatever you can to offload work from them and don't rush them for information or resources.
- We should do what we can to make things at no cost to the partner. When that isn't possible we should only ask the org to front cost of components.
